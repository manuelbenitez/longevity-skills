# Walnuts

Cited by both Fontana's *The Path to Longevity* and Longo's *The Longevity Diet*
as a daily-staple nut. Both books converge on roughly the same dose (around an
ounce a day) and roughly the same reason (alpha-linolenic acid, polyphenols, and
arginine-rich plant protein), though they frame it differently.

## Why It Matters for Longevity

A handful of walnuts a day shows up in two independent longitudinal studies as
one of the cleanest dietary signals for lower all-cause mortality. The Harvard
Nurses' Health Study tracked 118,962 adults over 30 years and saw a 14% reduction
in mortality and a 25% reduction in cardiovascular death among daily-walnut
eaters (Bao et al., NEJM 2013). The PREDIMED substudy showed walnut + olive oil
together produced a stronger lipid-profile improvement than either alone.

The mechanism is well-characterized. Walnuts are the only common nut with
meaningful ALA omega-3 content (~9g per 100g). ALA reduces hepatic VLDL output,
lowers LDL, and feeds anti-inflammatory eicosanoid pathways. The ellagic acid
gets converted by gut bacteria into urolithins, which modulate mitochondrial
function. The arginine supports endothelial NO production.

## How to Use Them

**Daily dose, both books agree:** about an ounce (roughly 7 whole walnuts, or
28-30g). Fontana emphasizes whole-food consumption — eat them as a snack or
sprinkle on salads. Longo frames the same dose as part of a low-protein,
plant-forward longevity pattern and explicitly recommends them as one of the
preferred protein sources for adults over 65.

**Preparation matters more than people realize.** ALA degrades above ~160°C.
Roasted walnuts at high heat lose most of the omega-3 benefit. Use them raw, or
toast briefly at low heat (5 minutes at 150°C) if you want the deeper flavor.
Refrigerate after opening — the fats oxidize at room temperature within weeks.

## What to Pair Them With

| Pairing | Why |
|---------|-----|
| Olive oil | ALA + MUFA together beat either alone (PREDIMED substudy) |
| Blue cheese | Classic French pairing; sulfur compounds in cheese complement walnut bitterness |
| Dark chocolate | Polyphenol stacking — both rich in flavonoids and ellagic-acid derivatives |

## What the Books Disagree On

Fontana frames walnuts primarily as a Mediterranean staple and emphasizes the
omega-3 angle for cardiovascular protection. Longo emphasizes their role as a
plant-protein source that keeps IGF-1 lower than animal-protein equivalents.
Both views are consistent; they're just looking at the same nut through
different mechanistic lenses.
